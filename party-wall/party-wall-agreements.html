What is a Party Wall Agreement?

At Survey one, a party wall agreement governs the shared walls or structures that separate neighboring properties, typically in terraced or semi-detached houses. These walls are often the boundary between two properties, such as a garden wall or a common wall between two adjoining houses.

The Party Wall Act of 1996 regulates the process, ensuring that all parties are informed and protected before construction begins. Whether you’re the property owner undertaking the work or a neighboring owner, understanding the nuances of a party wall agreement is key.

When Do You Need a Party Wall Agreement?

If you’re planning construction that could affect a party wall whether you’re adding a new wall or altering an existing one you’ll likely need a Party Wall Agreement in Chigwell. Under the Party Wall Act, any work that may impact a shared wall or boundary requires formal notice to your neighbors. This notice allows them to review the proposed work and respond accordingly. In the case of a dispute, a party wall surveyor can help mediate and ensure that an agreement is reached.

Not every job requires a full party wall agreement, though. For instance, minor tasks like installing kitchen cabinets, hanging shelves, or replacing electrical wiring may not require an agreement. However, if the work involves drilling into or altering a shared wall, a formal agreement is essential to avoid future disputes.

Why Do You Need a Party Wall Agreement?

The purpose of a party wall agreement is to protect both property owners’ rights and ensure that construction work doesn’t negatively affect the neighboring property. Before you start any work that involves a party wall, you must serve a Party Wall Notice to your neighbors. This notice gives them the chance to consent to the proposed work or raise any concerns. Giving adequate notice—anywhere from two months to a year—helps prevent misunderstandings and gives both parties ample time to address potential issues.

Meeting with your neighbors in person before serving the notice is a good way to build rapport and ensure transparency. It can go a long way toward resolving concerns and avoiding conflict later on. You can also direct your neighbors to the government’s Party Wall Act section to help them better understand the process.

How to Serve a Party Wall Notice

To serve a Party Wall Notice, you must provide a detailed explanation of the work you plan to undertake, the anticipated start date, and any requests for access to the neighboring property (such as for materials or equipment). This notice must be delivered to both property owners and, if applicable, tenants of adjacent leasehold properties.

While sending out a formal notice can seem like a daunting task, it doesn’t have to be. Our surveyors can assist you with drafting and sending the proper notice. We’ll also provide a return envelope for your neighbors to sign and return. If you’ve already spoken with your neighbors about the work, this should come as no surprise, and they will likely be more understanding.

Do You Need a Party Wall Surveyor?

In some cases, you may not need a party wall surveyor if your neighbor agrees to the proposed work in writing. However, even if you don’t think you need a surveyor, it’s always a good idea to have one on hand to ensure that the work is done correctly and any potential damage to the shared wall is documented. Taking photographs or videos of the existing condition of the wall before starting work can help protect you in case of any disputes later.

If your neighbor objects to the proposed work, a party wall surveyor will be necessary to help draft a Party Wall Award, which outlines the specifics of the work to be done, how it will be carried out, and the responsibilities of both parties involved. Often, the same surveyor will represent both property owners, which can help minimize costs and streamline the process.

What Happens If My Neighbor Objects?

If your neighbor disagrees with your proposed construction, you’ll need to work with a party wall surveyor to prepare a Party Wall Award. This legal document outlines the agreed-upon terms of the work and protects both parties’ interests. The surveyor will also ensure that the work adheres to the Party Wall Act and that the neighboring property is protected.
